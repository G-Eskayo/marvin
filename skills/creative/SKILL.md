# Creative

Produce creative output that is genuinely unexpected — not just competent.

## The Core Problem

The default creative response is always the most predictable one: the obvious metaphor, the safe structure, the genre cliche, the first idea that comes to mind. That default is almost always wrong for creative work.

This skill forces divergence before convergence.

## Process

### 1. Resist the first idea

Whatever comes to mind immediately — note it, then set it aside. It's the predictable answer. The first idea is usually:
- The genre default
- The safest interpretation of the brief
- What everyone else would also produce

Generate at least 3 radically different directions before choosing.

### 2. Reframe the brief

Before generating, ask:
- What is the *actual* goal underneath the stated request?
- What would this look like if the constraints were inverted?
- What unexpected medium, form, or angle could carry this?
- What's the most interesting *wrong* interpretation?

The best creative work often solves a different problem than the one stated.

### 3. Apply constraints deliberately

Constraints produce creativity, not freedom. When stuck:
- Add an arbitrary rule (must use no adjectives, must be under 10 words, must be a dialogue)
- Steal the structure from an unrelated domain (write this like a legal brief / a recipe / a weather report)
- Force a specific emotional register that feels wrong for the topic
- Impose a perspective shift (who else could narrate this?)

### 4. Generate divergent options

For any creative task, produce multiple genuinely different directions — not variations on the same idea. Different means:
- Different form or structure
- Different emotional tone
- Different underlying metaphor or framing
- Different intended effect on the audience

Present them without over-explaining. Let the work speak. Brief rationale only if asked.

### 5. Iterate, don't perfect

Creative work improves through iteration, not polish. A rough but surprising idea beats a polished safe one. Show the direction, get feedback, sharpen.

Don't over-explain creative choices. If it needs a paragraph of justification, it isn't working.

## Quality Standards

Good creative output:
- Has a specific, non-generic quality — something only *this* piece could do
- Creates an unexpected effect (surprise, tension, resonance, humor that lands)
- Doesn't explain itself
- Leaves room for the reader/viewer to complete the meaning

Bad creative output:
- Could have been generated by anyone
- Uses the first metaphor that comes to mind
- Explains what it's trying to do instead of doing it
- Optimizes for "safe and competent" over "surprising and alive"

## Domain-Specific Notes

**Writing**: Voice and rhythm matter more than correctness. Unusual sentence structure, unexpected word choice, and white space are tools.

**Naming / branding**: Avoid descriptive names. The best names are oblique, memorable, and create the right feeling without stating it.

**Ideation / brainstorming**: Quantity first, quality second. Suspend judgment during generation. The weird idea often contains the seed of the right one.

**Problem-solving**: Treat constraints as creative prompts, not blockers. "We can't do X" often means "here's an interesting design problem."

## What Not to Do

- Don't optimize for approval ("here's a safe version everyone will like")
- Don't explain the joke / metaphor / concept
- Don't mistake complexity for depth
- Don't confuse "different" with "difficult to understand"
- Don't iterate toward blandness — preserve the surprising quality through revision
